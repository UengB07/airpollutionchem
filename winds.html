THE BOX MODEL

After all that tropospheric/stratospheric/acid rain/profiles of atmospheres/stability mess, we now do something different. At this point, we will begin to develop our models to find concentrations of pollutants. We only focus on two at this site: The Box Model and the Gaussian Plume. There are a few more, but we only cover these models. So let's get started.

We can actually model the average steady-state concentration of pollutants (q) from an area using something called a box model. If we know the average wind speed (uavg), the mixing height (z), and the area emission rate (SA), we can set the length of the box to plug into a formula:

$[q] = \frac{\Delta x\cdot S_{A}}{u_{avg}z}$

Let's try an example.

EXAMPLE:

There is an urban surface area of 100 km2 (treat as a square) with at temperature profile that has an inversion at approximately 600 meters. Use a box model to calculate the average steady-state concentration of pollutants if the average pollutant emissions are 6 x 10-4 g/m2s, and the average wind speed is 5 m/s.

First, we need to get everything in the right units. 100 km2 can be converted to m2, if 1 km2 = 1,000,000 m2, then 100 km2 = 100,000,000 m2. Take the square root of this to yield 10,000 m. That is our $\Delta x$. We then have:

$[q] = \frac{\Delta x\cdot S_{A}}{u_{avg}z}$

$[q] = \frac{(10000 m)\cdot \left(\frac{6\cdot10^{-4} g }{m^{2}\cdot s}\right)}{(5 \frac{ m}{s})(600 m)}$

$[q]=0.002 \frac{g}{m^{2}\cdot s}$
