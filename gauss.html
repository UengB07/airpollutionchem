The Gaussian Plume

A better model to calculate the concentration of pollutants which is more sophisticated than the box model is the Gaussian Plume Model. The Gaussian Plume Model functions by using concepts of statistics, physics, and other stuff. Take a glance at the horrendous picture below (just a glance, don't analyze it in strict detail):

WHAT IN THE WORLD IS THAT?

Well, it's an outline of the Gaussian Plume equation. It is some very big scary looking equation that estimates pollutant concentrations at a certain point from an emissions source. The basic (which doesn't seem so basic) formula for the Gaussian Plume is

$C(x,y,z) = \left(\frac{Q}{U}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_{y}\sigma_{z}}\right) e^{\left(\frac{-y^{2}}{2\sigma_{y}^{2}}\right)}\left[e^{\left(\frac{-(z-H)^{2}}{2\sigma_{z}^{2}}\right)} + e^{\left(\frac{-(z+H)^{2}}{2\sigma_{z}^{2}}\right)} \right].$

This is the main equation of this page (and a rather intimidating one at that), so let's break it down.

We start with a smokestack that is developing a plume. Look at 1) and the green box. There are 3 axes - the x-axis, which is left to right, the y-axis, which is point toward you and away from you, and the z-axis, which stands for the altitude. The green box shows what happens in the y-axis. The concentration is the greatest in the center line, while y and $\sigma_{y}$ respresent the deviation of how far the plume travels along the y-axis. There is wind speed blowing, and the formula modelers derived is that green formula in the black box in the #1 dotted green box (yes we have boxes in boxes).

We can then do something similar to the z-axis, but there becomes a problem. We have to adjust the z-axis formula to (account for the source being raised from the ground - the default value of altitude where the concentrations emit usually doesn't begin at zero). Look at the left side in the picture. We set H = hs (the stack height) + he (the max height at which the plume rises). This is inputted into the equation and modifies it. We must also account for every single pollutant not being deposited on the ground - so the modelers used a "reflection" metaphor to account for this. You can see a mirror image across of the surface line. They also accounted for this by adding an additional term, but instead of inputting z - H, it's z + H (hence, the reflection). See the red equation after the adjustment (Look at 2).

When we get all this, we get the Gaussian Plume equation.

$C(x,y,z) = \left(\frac{Q}{U}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_{y}\sigma_{z}}\right) e^{\left(\frac{-y^{2}}{2\sigma_{y}^{2}}\right)}\left[e^{\left(\frac{-(z-H)^{2}}{2\sigma_{z}^{2}}\right)} + e^{\left(\frac{-(z+H)^{2}}{2\sigma_{z}^{2}}\right)} \right],\:where$ (!)

  • $Q=emission\:rate$
  • $u=wind\:speed$
  • $\sigma_{y}=y\:coefficient$
  • $\sigma_{z}=z\:coefficient$
  • $y=length\:of\:interest\:of\:y$
  • $z=height$
  • $H=stack\:of\:height + plume\:height$

To find the y and z coefficients (also known as dispersion coefficients), you will need a manual. On this page, the necessary coefficients will be given to you. You can look up dispersion coefficients online (search "Gaussian Plume dispersion coefficents"). For instance, the page http://www.mne.psu.edu/cimbala/me433/Lectures/Tables_for_Gaussian_Plume_Model.pdf has an example of how to find it when it's not given. The coefficients vary with insolation, wind speed, clouds...the value honestly depends on the meterological conditions.

EXAMPLE: Calculate the expected concentration near the ground (z=0) if the wind speed (u) is 4 m/s, $\sigma_{y}$ = 150 m, $\sigma_{z}$ = 90 m (these coefficients, if you look in a reference table, should coincide with a moderately sunny day, probably Class B), and the stack height is 50 meters where the plume rises 30 m above the plume. Assume we have no interest in the y-axis (cross-axis), and let y = 0. The emission rate (Q) is 140 g/s.

So H = Hs + He = 50 m + 30 m = 80 m, and:

$C(x,y,z) = \left(\frac{Q}{U}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_{y}\sigma_{z}}\right) e^{\left(\frac{-y^{2}}{2\sigma_{y}^{2}}\right)}\left[e^{\left(\frac{-(z-H)^{2}}{2\sigma_{z}^{2}}\right)} + e^{\left(\frac{-(z+H)^{2}}{2\sigma_{z}^{2}}\right)} \right]$

$C(x,y,z) = \left(\frac{140\:g/s}{4\:m/s}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2\pi(150\:m)(90\:m)}\right) e^{\left(\frac{-0^{2}}{2(150\:m)^{2}}\right)}\left[e^{\left(\frac{-(0\:m-80\:m)^{2}}{2(90)^{2}}\right)} + e^{\left(\frac{-(0\:m+80\:m)^{2}}{2(90)^{2}}\right)} \right]$

$C(x,y,z) = 5.5 \cdot 10^{-4} g/m^{3}$
